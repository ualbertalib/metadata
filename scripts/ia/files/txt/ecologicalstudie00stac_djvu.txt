I introduction' 

The native vegetation of the prairie provinces has as 
yet received little attention irom the ecologist. Some 
work has heen done iDut considering the amoimt there is to 
he done it might he said that not more than a mere start has 
heen made. Jackson, of the Manitohe College of Agriculture, 
has studied the successions of the Red River Basin (10). 

Lewis and Bowiding^oi the Department of Botany, University 
of Alherta, have published a paper on ^'The vegetation and 
retrogressive changes of peat areas (muskegs) in Central 
Alherta” (192b). Apart from the findings of these invest¬ 
igations all that is known of the native ecology of the 
prairie provinces is based on the observation of a few bot¬ 
anists who have casually visited the district. Among these 
are Macoun, Malte, Clements and some of his co-workers of 
the Carnegie Institute of Washington, and possibly others.

The Purpose of the Investigation. 

In 1925 under the direction of the Department of Field Hus- 
handry of the University of Alherta and the Dominion Experiment¬ 
al Farm System a survey of one of these areas, the Grande-Prairie- 
Beaverlodge district was undertaken* The ultimate aim of this 
survey is to determine the agricultural possibilities of the 
district as indicated by the natural vegetation. In order to 
determine the significance of the native vegetation the ecology 
of the area must be worked out and the findings related to those 
of other districts where the relation of climate and natural 
vegetation to agriculture has been conclusively studied. 

Since a complete study of the district involves a project 
of considerable magnitude it was decided to limit the present 
undertaking to an investigation of the climatic and ecological 
relations of the native grasses*

Drainage• 

The district is drained by the Wapiti river and its tribu¬ 
taries. S^st of Grande Prairie the Wapiti flows into the Smoky 
which, in turn empties into the Peace Siver near the town of 
Peace River. The Red Willow river enters the district from 
the west and after following a winding course flows into the 
Wapiti about 12 miles south of Beaverlodge. The Beaverlodge 
river, usually not more than a good sized creek, flows through 
several lakes at the north-west corner of the section and from 
there in a general south-easterly direction until it joins the 
Red Willow a few miles before the latter joins the Wapitis The 
only other stream of much importance is Bear Creek. This runs 
parallel to the Beaverlodge river and drains the north-east por¬ 
tion. After passing through Bear Lake and Grande Prairie it 
flows into the 7 /apiti. 

Numerous lakes dot the country. The most important of 
these are Bear Lake and Lake Saskatoon*.... There are many others 
but they are not sufficiently important to warrant mention. 

As shown in Figure 5 they are v/idely scattered and together 
with the intermittent streams which take care of the melted 
snow in the spring have an important bearing on the floral as¬ 
pect of the section.

The Vegetation* 

As mentioned above this area lies within a forest formation, 
possibly the northern mixed forest since the vegetation seems to 
resemble this rather than the Cordillerian forest. This forma¬ 
tion crosses the Wapiti at the south. In general its eastern 
boundary corresponds to the line of the 2b00 foot contour at the 
west end of the district. Within the district, centering on 
Saskatoon Mountain, it covers about three townships. The remain¬ 
der is parkland and presents a decided contrast to the forest form¬ 
ation* The exact relationship of these two is very indefinite, 
and depends on local topography and moisture conditions. Along 
each creek or lake are successions of spruce, poplar, willow, and 
grassland. In Figure 5 ^he central portion is predominantly 
grassland while the portion between this ana the forest formation 
is covered in varying degrees with poplar or willow.

Effectiveness of Precipitation

effectiveness. Factors such as temperature, relative humidity, 
wind movement, etc., have a definite influence on the amount

can he used hy the plant. The amount of precipitation that 
produces "true prairie" conditions in Alherta would produce 
only "short grass" or"desert scrub" in the Great Plains.

7 /hile the ecological significance of this data stated in terms

The evaporating power of the Chinook winds in the southern
